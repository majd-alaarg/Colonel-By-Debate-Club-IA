About the Club

Colonel By Debate Club is ‘Arguably the Best’ club at Colonel By. Debate club helps you develop skills, from rhetorical strategies, to public speaking and confidence. Our weekly club hosts British Parliamentary debate rounds that cover relevant and specialized topics. Additionally, we host seminars and workshops to help you learn new skills, and develop your existing ones.

We also participate in tournaments hosted by universities such as UOttawa and McGill as well as attending regional, provincial and national tournaments where we compete against others from across the province and the country! Our club is open to first-timers, as well as experienced debaters. Your first debate can often be intimidating, but our club fosters a welcoming and friendly environment. Swing by for a fun afternoon of debating and learning.

Introduction to British Parliamentary Debate

A complete guide to debating BP rounds competitively.

What is British Parliamentary debate? (1/4)

An overview

British Parliamentary (BP) debate style is one of the most used styles of debate in Canada. It is also used internationally. As such, it is important to get a good grip on it.

British Parliamentary Rounds are the most commonly practiced during Colonel By Debate Club's practice meetings

A BP debate round consists of one motion debated by two sides. On each side, there are two separate teams of two debaters each, all of which have five-minute speeches.

The Structure of a BP Round (2/4)

How it all works

British Parliamentary debate rounds consist of 2 sides, Government/Proposition who argues for the motion and Opposition who argues against the motion.

During most debate rounds, you are put on a random side. You will not always debate from a perspective or side that you particularly agree with.

In the round, there are four teams:

  • Opening Government (OG)First team on side government
  • Opening Opposition (OO)First team on side opposition
  • Closing Government (CG)Second team on side government
  • Closing Opposition (CO)Second team on side opposition

The Speakers Within a Debate (3/4)

The 4 Speakers of the Front Half

1. Prime Minister (PM): First overall speaker, first speaker on government side, first speaker on team OG

2. Leader of Opposition (LO): Second overall speaker, first speaker on opposition side, first speaker on team OO

3. Deputy Prime Minister (DPM): Third overall speaker, second speaker on government side, second speaker on team OG

4. Deputy Leader of Opposition (DLO): Fourth overall speaker, second speaker on opposition side, second speaker on team OO

The Speakers Within a Debate (4/4)

The 4 Speakers of the Back Half

5. Member of Government (MG): Fifth overall speaker, third speaker on government side, first speaker on team CG

6. Member of Opposition (MO: Sixth overall speaker, third speaker on opposition side, first speaker on team CG

7. Government Whip (GW): Seventh overall speaker, last speaker on government side, second speaker on team CG

8. Opposition Whip (GW): Last overall speaker, last speaker on opposition side, second speaker on team CO

What is Canadian Parliamentary debate? (1/3)

An overview

Canadian Parliamentary is a debate style occasionally seen in Canadian high school and university debate tournaments. CP is becoming more frequently replaced by BP, but it's still useful to know how to debate in this style.

Most national and provincial competitions in Canada function around the Canadian Parliamentary Round format.

The entirety of CP is much like the front half of a BP debate. Like a British Parliamentary debate, a CP round consists of one motion debated by two sides. However, instead of four teams like a BP round, Canadian Parliamentary rounds have two teams, one on each side.

The Structure of a BP Round (2/3)

How it all works

Canadian Parliamentary debate rounds consist of 2 sides, the Government, who argues for the motion, and Opposition, who argues against the motion.

Canadian Parliamentary rounds have two teams arguing two different sides, each team has two members for a total of four debaters in the round.

The timing of CP rounds differs from BP debates with individual debaters having longer speeches and the delivery of whip speech being flexible in terms of conjoined Crown-Whip Speeches.

The Speakers Within the Debate (3/3)

The 4 Speakers of a CP Round

1. Prime Minister (PM): First overall speaker and first speaker on government team, presents a short whip speech (also known as a crux or a reply speech) at the very end of the debate. (7 minute speech + 3 minute whip)

2. Leader of Opposition (LO): Second overall speaker and first speaker on opposition team, presents a short whip speech after the DLO's speech. (7 minute speech + 3 minute whip)

3. Minister of the Crown (MC): Third overall speaker and second speaker on government team. (7 minute speech)

4. Member of Opposition (MO): Fourth overall speaker and second speaker on opposition team. (7 minute speech)

Introduction to Debate Motions

A complete guide to the different types of competitive debate motions.

What are debate motions?

An overview

A motion is a proposal that government wants to pass and opposition wants to fail (i.e. 'This house would ban alcohol"). The house (abbreviated as TH) is the entity, be it a person, a state, or an organization, that would act on the motion (i.e. in "TH would ban alcohol," TH might be the government of Canada, in which case the motion should be read as "The government of Canada would ban alcohol")

Learning the different topics and having a general idea on how to debate them is massively helpful.

It is likely that you will encounter a variety of motions that start with different abbreviations. The most common examples include: THW, THBT, THR, THP, THS and THO.

THW - "This House Would"

What is THW?
  • A tangible action will be taken.
  • The model should explain who will be completing the action ("the house") and how this action will be done.
  • The method in which the motion will be completed is important and can be debated on.
  • Ex. "THW lower the voting age" = "This house" is Canada, the voting age for all elections (federal, provincial, municipal) will be lowered to 16.

THBT - "This House Believes That"

What is THBT?
  • Debaters want to convince the judge to hold a certain opinion.
  • Note that THBT rounds aren't about actual action taking place in the real world, so the mechanism of how a change might be achieved is not nearly as relevant as the impact that the change will have.
  • The house is often modelled as a "rational individual."
  • Ex. "THBT it is better to be safe than sorry", "THBT UN Security Council should be abolished."

THR - "This House Regrets"

What is THR?
  • The goal is to convince the judge to regret something that has occurred in the past.
  • Usually this addresses the current results of the regretted thing, but can also include the past effects of it.
  • Ex. "THR the commodification of feminism", "THR the narrative of the one true love."

THP - "This House Prefers"

What is THP?
  • Typically featuring a hypothetical scenario that government wants to prove is better than the status quo (the current world) and that opposition wants to prove is worse.
  • In these motions, it's important to worldbuild, which means to explain how the world works and how the world differs from the status quo.
  • Ex. "THP a world in which all humans share the same language", "THP a world in which children are raised to aspire to be single as opposed to married."

THS/THO - "This House Supports/Opposes"

What is THW?
  • The purpose of this motion is to convince the judge to either side with or side against a movement, organization, person, concept, etc.
  • This is between THW and THBT in terms of real-world impact.
  • While the house isn't actively enacting anything, they are actively supporting someone or something which may change the world.
  • Ex. "THS military intervention in Venezuela", "THO the ownership of pets."
